McCain the choice for freedom, prosperity


SECTION: Pg. 08


LENGTH: 721 words


Americans will choose today between two clear and distinct agendas. John McCain proposes more freedom, choice and prosperity. Barack Obama offers less of each.

John McCain would rejuvenate America's moribund economy by halving the capital gains and dividend taxes from 15 percent to 7.5, chopping the globally uncompetitive corporate tax from 35 percent to 25, and letting today's 45 percent death tax settle at 15 percent on estates above $5 million. He also has proposed letting Americans choose either today's 67,204-page tax code or a voluntary flatter, simpler tax with two rates of perhaps 25 and 15 percent.

Obama would stuff a pillow over the face of America's bedridden economy by boosting capital gains and dividend taxes from 15 percent to 20. He would hike top income tax rates from 33 and 35 percent to 36 and 39.6 percent. He also would maintain the 35 percent corporate tax and a 45 percent death tax on estates above $3.5 million.

Obama promises "tax cuts" to 95 percent of Americans, even though 44 percent of filers pay $0 in income taxes. A shifting target of taxpayers would finance these transfer payments.

"If you make $250,000 a year or less, we will not raise your taxes," Obama declared last July 8. "We will cut your taxes."

On Oct. 25, Obama's threshold slipped. "If you have a job, pay taxes and make less than $200,000 a year, you'll get a tax cut," he said in a commercial.

VP nominee Joe Biden told Scranton, Pa.'s WNEP last week that tax cuts "should go to middle-class people -- people making under $150,000 a year."

"It's interesting how their definition of rich has a way of creeping down," McCain said last month in Pennsylvania.

Wherever Obama's vacuum nozzle lands, he should unplug it. Tax hikes are foolish in boom times and idiotic when the gross domestic product shrinks 0.3 percent, as it did last quarter.

McCain would revolutionize health care by establishing an individual market, so Americans could own and control their insurance. They could buy across state lines and transport coverage from job to job, to school, to home-based business or wherever. Americans could stop keeping thankless positions at thoughtless companies solely to remain insured. McCain would offer $2,500 refundable tax credits for individuals and $5,000 for families to help purchase coverage that suits their needs, not The Man's.

Obama offers a potpourri of paternalistic options, from keeping employer-provided coverage to buying a government-run plan from his National Health Insurance Exchange.

"Insurance sold in the Exchange would be guaranteed issue and community rated," warns National Center for Policy Analysis president John Goodman. "This means people would be able to wait until after they get sick to insure, and they would be able to do so without any financial penalty."

Coverage costs have soared in states that impose these unforgiving regulations.

"This is America, the most resourceful country on Earth," VP nominee Sarah Palin told Toledo, Ohio, voters last Wednesday in a superb energy-policy address. "We can drill, and refine, and mine, enrich, reprocess, invent, build, conserve, grow and use every available means to regain our independence (on energy)."

McCain-Palin's "all of the above" approach would include fast-tracking 45 atomic power plants for construction by 2030. McCain also has promised Iowa corn farmers that they can kiss goodbye their costly, distorted, environmentally ruinous ethanol program.

Obama wants a windfall profits tax -- a disincentive to new oil production. He says he now favors offshore drilling. But watch Democrats exhume the just-expired offshore-drilling moratorium. Obama even sponsored legislation to prohibit expanded high-tech maps and measurements of sub-oceanic hydrocarbons. He also would increase mandated ethanol production 67 percent by 2030 -- never mind the global chaos ethanol triggered by turbo-charging corn prices.

With the economy gasping, America needs greater freedom, broader choices, lower taxes and cheaper energy. John McCain's congruent policies will spur prosperity. Barack Obama's contrary proposals will stymie it.

Up or down?

You decide.

Early signs point to big turnout in NH


BYLINE: By HOLLY RAMER, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: POLITICAL NEWS


LENGTH: 966 words


Long early lines at some New Hampshire polling places suggested that the predicted heavy turnout for Tuesday's election is happening.

In Manchester, where officials said they expected turnout to exceed the 94 percent in the last presidential election, a worker stepped outside the Ward 2 polling place just before the 6 a.m. opening to see a line of about 100 stretching around a curve and into the parking lot.

"My God, look at the people," he said.

Machinist Nicholas Diaz, 44, showed up almost an hour early to be first in line. He voted for Republicans in the top races, though he said he trusts both presidential candidates to do right.

Small-business owner Robert Tubbs, 52, chose Democrat Barack Obama, who would be the first black president.

"It's easily the proudest vote I ever made," he said.

"It's huge. It opens up this country for everyone."

McCain, whose presidential hopes were resurrected in the 2000 and 2008 New Hampshire presidential primaries, was hoping that Tuesday would be a replay of this year's primary: a upset victory for him and a painful and unexpected defeat for Obama.

McCain sounded more like a lovesick teenager than a 72-year-old presidential candidate last month when he told a New Hampshire audience, "I love you. ... I'm asking you to come out one more time."

Obama, meanwhile, has been warning against overconfidence, citing his New Hampshire primary loss to Sen. Hillary Rodham Clinton in January despite polls showing him with a solid lead.

"For those who are getting a little cocky, I've got two words for you: New Hampshire," he said last month.

Excitement over the presidential race somewhat overshadowed the state's other races, including the bitter rematch between Republican Sen. John Sununu and Democrat Jeanne Shaheen. Sununu portrays the former governor as a "taxing machine" in one ad; she portrays him as President Bush's equally-evil twin.

Six years ago, when Bush was still popular, Sununu defeated Shaheen by 4 percentage points. This time around, Shaheen had the twin advantages of Obama's coattails and demographic shifts that have boosted Democrats. A recent study by the University of New Hampshire estimated that about one-quarter of the electorate has changed since 2002, with both young voters and newcomers to the state more likely to identify themselves as Democrats.

Registered independent David Lamarre-Vincent, executive director of the New Hampshire Council of Churches, illustrated the closeness of the Senate race outside the polls in Concord Tuesday morning.

"It's a real close call for the Senate seat because in dealing with John Sununu in the last couple year, he has been accessible and a critical listener, and when he doesn't agree with you, he will just tell you in a non-hostile fashion. On the war, he's voted several times in the wrong way, but he'll still talk to you.

"I'll probably make this decision when I'm in the booth."

On the issues, Shaheen, 61, of Madbury, has pushed for making health care more accessible and affordable, ending the war in Iraq and promoting alternative energy. But she focused much of her energy on trying to link Sununu to the unpopular president.

Sununu, 44, of Waterville Valley, has cast himself as a leader on the housing crisis, the economy and the environment who has stood up to his party when necessary to protect taxpayers and civil liberties.

In the 1st Congressional District, first-term Democrat Shea-Porter was in a rematch with former Rep. Jeb Bradley, the Republican she defeated in 2006. The Iraq war was the top issue that year, and the nationwide wave of anti-Republican sentiment helped carry Shea-Porter's low-budget, grass-roots campaign to victory.

This year, the two have sparred over how to bolster the economy, fix the lending crisis and control federal budget deficits.

Bradley, 56, of Wolfeboro, has criticized Shea-Porter for voting to reverse Bush's tax cuts for upper income Americans and says any tax increase during tough economic times will further slow the economy. Shea-Porter, 55, of Rochester, favors cutting taxes for small businesses and the middle class, while eliminating tax breaks for big corporations.

In the 2nd Congressional District, first-term Democrat Paul Hodes faced political newcomer Jennifer Horn, a Republican who argued that smaller government and lower taxes are the key to economic health. Though she has worked in the health insurance and media industries, Horn, 44, of Nashua, said raising five children has provided her with all the experience she needs to understand the problems facing the nation.

Hodes, 57, of Concord, pointed to examples of how he has not only understood but solved some of those problems. He counts as his major achievements creating a commission to steer economic development and federal funding to northern New Hampshire, and Michelle's Law, which guarantees that sick college students won't lose their health insurance if they leave school to seek treatment.

In the quietest major race, both Democratic Gov. John Lynch and his Republican challenger, state Sen. Joe Kenney, pitched themselves as the best one to steer the state through tough economic times over the next two years.

Lynch, 55, of Hopkinton, points to his two terms as proof of his ability to manage state finances without an income or sales tax, and says the budget cuts he has made already show voters that he will make more as needed to keep the budget balanced.

Kenney, 48, of Wakefield, argues that Lynch has led the state on a spending spree that will transform frugal New Hampshire and force enactment of an income or sales tax. But voters either haven't heard Kenney's argument or aren't buying it: polls showed Lynch far ahead.

Libertarian candidates are on the ballot in all the top races, and independent Ralph Nader was on the presidential ballot.

Political polls sometimes provide a cloudy crystal ball


BYLINE: MICHAEL COUSINEAU, New Hampshire Sunday News


SECTION: Pg. 06


LENGTH: 985 words


New Hampshire Sunday News

If you believe recent election polls, Democratic presidential nominee Barack Obama is ahead in New Hampshire by 25 ... by 18 ... by 11 ... by 5 ...by 4.

Or maybe not at all -- if you factor in the margin of error.

"It's like watching the stock market these days," said state GOP Party Chairman Fergus Cullen.

Bombarded by polls in newspapers and on television and the Internet, voters often take away what they want from them, according to voters, pollsters and political observers.

"The ones that show them close are the ones I believe," said Bedford's Walter Scott, lunching Thursday at J.W. Hill's Sports Bar & Grille in Manchester.

Pollsters agree on their greatest challenge.

"That's the biggest problem you're having in election polling: who's going to show up," said Andy Smith, director of the University of New Hampshire Survey Center.

Two UNH polls released last week showed different party breakdowns.

One poll, conducted from Oct. 25 to last Monday, had 42 percent of respondents say they were registered undeclared, 31 percent Democratic and 27 percent Republican. That poll had Obama leading, 58 to 33 percent, over Republican nominee John McCain, with a 3.8 percentage point margin of error.

The second poll, conducted Monday through Wednesday, had 40 percent registered undeclared, 31 percent Republican and 29 percent Democratic. That poll pegged the race 54 to 36 percent, with a 3.9 percentage point margin of error.

On Friday, Manchester pollster Dick Bennett released a poll that had 35 percent registered undeclareds, 33 percent Republicans and 32 percent Democrats. His poll had Obama ahead, 56 to 41 percent, with a 4 percentage point margin of error.

"Polls are a rough guesstimate of what the election is at any given point in time," Smith said. "It's a fuzzy snapshot."

Neither pollster weights his poll by a predetermined percentage of voters based on party registration.

"Part of the reason is, we don't make any assumptions of what the electorate's going to look like," Smith said.

Smith said research done by him and two UNH colleagues shows that about a third of all potential voters are new to the state's electoral process since 2000, many of them recent arrivals from Massachusetts.

"Those new voters are about 10 percent more Democratic than the established voters in the past," he said.

If you think there are more polls this year, you would be correct.

And Web sites, such as realclearpolitics.com and fivethirtyeight.com, act as clearinghouses for the explosion.

But averaging all the polls in a given state doesn't necessarily give voters an accurate portrayal of what's going on politically, according to Jennifer Donahue, political director at the New Hampshire Institute of Politics at St. Anselm College.

"You can't just take a whole zoo of animals and put them all in one cage and expect them to work out together," said Donahue, who has appeared frequently on MSNBC and CNN this election cycle.

Bennett said human nature takes hold when viewing polls.

"Most people who are interested in polls have their own biases and are looking for polls that support their biases," he said.

Smith said every household in the state with a telephone land line has an equal chance of being selected. And when someone picks up a phone, he or she is asked to turn it over to the adult with the most recent birthday to keep the sample random, Smith said.

He said it's "getting harder and harder" to get people to answer the phone or agree to be surveyed, but poll callers will try up to eight times to reach specific homes.

A frequent complaint is that pollsters don't reach cell-phone users.

Bennett said his firm's polling includes numbers assigned to cell phones. Smith said research shows little difference in the overall voting results when exclusive cell users are mixed with land-line users. And New Hampshire has a small percentage of exclusively cell households, he said.

A survey of exclusive cell-phone users by the Pew Research Center in June showed respondents supported Obama over McCain 61 to 32 percent. But when land-line and cell users were combined, the spread became a 48-40 Obama advantage. A standard land-line survey showed the race at 46-41 Obama.

In January, voters and candidates received a cautionary lesson on polling. Some TV commentators and reporters had been writing the political obituary for Obama's main Democratic rival, Sen. Hillary Clinton, based on polling prior to the New Hampshire Primary. A Zogby poll released on primary day had her trailing by 13 points.

Clinton topped Obama, 39 to 37 percent.

There's a "lag time when events occur in a campaign and when polls pick them up," Smith said.

The Iowa caucuses took place on a Thursday, followed by a New Hampshire debate Saturday night and Clinton's emotional meeting with voters in Portsmouth on Monday before Granite Staters voted Tuesday.

As recently as the Sunday before the primary, Smith's UNH poll cautioned that about 20 percent of those expecting to vote in the Democratic primary remained undecided.

Looking back on the Democratic primary, state Democratic Party Chairman Ray Buckley said, "I think it gives people a healthy skepticism about the polls."

Donahue said pundits and reporters need to be careful to put polls in perspective, providing the margin of error and the number of undecideds rather than flatly stating Candidate X enjoys a 2 percentage point lead.

"I think horse races are fun at the track, but I think as much interest the press does have in polls, they have to be used responsibly," Donahue said.

In a state where one political season seemingly runs into the next, many voters, including Joe O'Connor of Windham, said they're fed up with polling, campaign commercials and phone calls.

Still, O'Connor checked the Internet for the latest poll updates before meeting a friend for lunch Thursday.

"I'm ready for it to be over, but I want to see where my guy's at," he said.

Democrats striving for lock on Senate control


BYLINE: By TOM RAUM, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: POLITICAL NEWS


LENGTH: 1100 words


DATELINE: WASHINGTON


A usually elusive 60-40 Senate majority is the Democrats' target in Tuesday's election. They are certain to make gains even if they fall short of those magic numbers needed to break Republican choke holds on their aqenda.

Democratic Senate candidates will benefit from any coattail effect a big Obama victory might bring, as well as from the already weak economy and an unpopular war.

Math also favors Democratic Senate candidates this year. With 35 of the Senate's 100 seats on the ballot Tuesday, Democrats have fewer at risk than do Republicans.

After picking up six seats in the 2006 midterm elections to wrest control from Republicans, Democrats have the barest of Senate majorities 51 seats under their control, including two occupied by independents.

But they are overwhelmingly favored to pick up GOP-held seats in Virginia, New Mexico and Colorado, three states where Republican senators are retiring.

And many Republican incumbents running for re-election are in difficult races. Included in that group is Ted Stevens of Alaska, the longest-serving Republican senator in history who was convicted earlier this week on seven corruption counts. He's high on the list of top Democratic pickup opportunities, even though Alaska has long been a solid Republican state. Stevens spurned pleas by John McCain and party leaders to step aside rather than stay in the race against Democratic challenger Mark Begich, the mayor of Anchorage.

Also on the list of endangered Republicans: Sens. Elizabeth Dole of North Carolina; John Sununu of New Hampshire; Norm Coleman of Minnesota; Gordon Smith of Oregon; Saxby Chambliss of Georgia; Roger Wicker of Mississippi and possibly even Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell of Kentucky.

No Democratic-held seats appear in jeopardy, and just one Democratic senator, Mary Landrieu of Louisiana, faces a challenge.

Republicans have spent heavily in Louisiana, and the GOP's candidate, state Treasurer John Kennedy, is within reach. But recent polls suggest otherwise.

National Democrats are taking full advantage of the Obama campaign's organizing skills and registration drives, especially in battleground states.

"We've worked hand in glove with the Obama campaign," said Sen. Chuck Schumer, D-N.Y., chairman of the National Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee.

As to a filibuster-proof majority, "It's hard to get to that 60 and I don't want to oversell it," Schumer said. "But I do think we're going to pick up a large number of seats and that's going to make Democrats very happy."

In a mirror reflection of that optimism, Republicans are glum. Sen. John Ensign, R-Nev., chairman of the National Republican Senatorial Committee, says there's no question that the top of the ticket is affecting Senate races, along with the financial crisis. "It's a fairly toxic atmosphere out there" for Republicans, he suggests.

He acknowledged that Democrats could still exercise firm control by just coming close to the 60 votes: "I think if the Democrats get to 57 or 58 seats, on a lot of issues they will be able to override a Senate filibuster, because they seem to be able to pick off a few Republicans on a lot of the particular issues."

The shifting political landscape is no more dramatic than in the South.

In North Carolina, Dole who served in both the Nixon and Reagan administrations and is the wife of 1996 GOP presidential candidate Bob Dole looked at first like a safe bet in her bid for a second term.

But revelations that she has spent little time in North Carolina in the past year have allowed Democratic newcomer Kay Hagan, a state senator with financial backing from national Democrats, to make the race one of the nation's most competitive. A late-in-the-race Dole ad suggesting her rival has ties to an atheist group may have backfired. Hagan, who is a Presbyterian Sunday school teacher, took offense and threatened to go to court for a "cease-and-desist" order.

In next-door Virginia, another red state won by Bush in 2000 and 2004, Obama has made an aggressive effort and is leading in recent polls. Former Democratic Gov. Mark Warner, a Senate candidate, has a commanding lead in polls over another former Virginia governor, Republican Jim Gilmore.

In Georgia, incumbent Republican Chambliss looked like another sure winner. But he's being forced into a tight race by Democrat Jim Martin, a former state representative.

What changed? A heavy Obama organizing campaign in the state, as in the rest of the South, has resulted in the registration of hundreds of thousands of new voters. In addition, Chambliss angered conservatives by voting for the $700 billion financial bailout bill.

While Chambliss appears slightly ahead, a spirited challenge from a third-party candidate could throw the election to a high-profile Dec. 2 runoff.

"This race looks quite interesting in a way that it didn't a month ago," said Merle Black, an Emory University political science professor who specializes in southern politics. If the race is forced into a runoff, "Georgia will be the center of the political universe for about a month," Black said.

Other close races:

Mississippi, where Wicker, the appointed GOP incumbent, is holding a slim lead to fill the remaining four years of the retired Trent Lott's term. Former Democratic Gov. Ronnie Musgrove is making the race more competitive than first anticipated.

Kentucky, where McConnell, the GOP Senate leader, is being heavily targeted by national Democrats. Although McConnell is favored to hold the seat, the Democratic pressure has kept him in his home state instead of helping other GOP candidates.

Minnesota, where Democrat Al Franken, the former "Saturday Night Live" writer and actor, appears to have pulled ahead of Republican Sen. Norm Coleman in a closely fought contest. In a recent development, a Texas businessman claimed in a lawsuit that he was forced by a political benefactor of Coleman to funnel $75,000 to an insurance company that employs Coleman's wife. Coleman on Friday denied the allegation as "false and defamatory."

The prospects that Obama might win the White House and Democrats gain an effective filibuster-proof majority is not lost on the nation's business community.

The U.S. Chamber of Commerce has poured $30 million into an issue-ads campaign arguing against what it considers would be an antibusiness Congress under a Senate firmly controlled by Democrats.

"I think that with a Democratic president, the focus on the Senate becomes even more pronounced, because the Senate will really be the arbiter of the final outcome of legislation," said Bill Miller, the chamber's political director.

NH Senate rematch may hinge on presidential race


BYLINE: By HOLLY RAMER, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: POLITICAL NEWS


LENGTH: 833 words


DATELINE: CONCORD N.H.


Given Jeanne Shaheen's aggressive advertising, you'd think Sen. John Sununu is George W. Bush.

The Democratic former governor, in a bitter rematch with the freshman Republican senator, rarely says one man's name without the other. A campaign commercial aired by national Democrats morphs the president's face into the senator's. One, Shaheen suggests, equals the other.

"John Sununu was with me from the beginning," Bush proclaims in a Shaheen ad. Six of 10 ads on her Web site either mention Bush or feature him with Sununu.

Aligning a Republican with the unpopular, lame duck president is as common this election year as the ups and downs of the stock market. But in New Hampshire, the only state in the country to vote for Bush in 2000 and switch to Democrat John Kerry in 2004, the tactic fits right in with the electorate's leftward trend.

It's working.

Shaheen leads Sununu by 6 percentage points, according to an Associated Press-GfK poll of 600 likely voters conducted Oct. 22-26 by landline telephone. Tellingly, only 14 percent of those in the state approve of the job Bush is doing and only 8 percent say the country is moving in the right direction.

It's a long way from their first matchup in 2002. Back then it was a good thing for Sununu to be aligned with Bush. The president was still basking in the political glow from his unwavering response to the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks. The economy was healthy and the war in Iraq was still on the planning table.

Meanwhile, Shaheen was struggling to find a politically acceptable way to overhaul the state's school funding system.

That November, Sununu beat her, 51 percent to 47 percent.

Fast-forward six years and their fortunes are almost reversed. McCain, who won the state's GOP presidential primaries in 2000 and 2008, is now struggling to wrest the state's four electoral votes from Democrat Barack Obama, sucking media attention from the Senate race.

"In any other cycle this would be a really exciting race because it is so close and they are combative," said Wayne L'Esperance, a political science professor at New England College, where the candidates interrupted and talked over each other during a recent debate. "But because we're so focused on this amazing presidential race with a very, very popular Democrat and John McCain, who's a favorite son of New Hampshire, I think a lot of folks have forgotten about this race."

New Hampshire's Senate race is one of the closest-watched in the country for what it says about the status of the GOP and the prospect that a Shaheen win might help Democrats close in on the 60-vote majority required to quash Republican filibusters.

Republicans across the country are arguing in the election's final days that voting Democrats into a governing majority would wreak further havoc on the economy and shut down checks and balances when they are most needed. Democrats counter that one-party rule might mean a quicker fix for the economy.

In New Hampshire, both candidates have strong name recognition and plenty of money, and they're both dogged, if not particularly dynamic, campaigners.

PORTSMOUTH -- At a time when public support is mounting for capping carbon dioxide on power plants and more federal regulations are expected, Powerspan Corp. is preparing to begin the first demonstrations of its ECO2 technology, a system that attaches to coal-fired power plant stacks and traps CO2.

RGGI caps the total amount of carbon that power plants in the 10-state region can pump out of their smokestacks at the current level 188 million tons. The initiative will gradually reduce carbon going into the atmosphere by lowering the cap in several steps, until it is 10 percent below the current level in 2018. During that 10-year span, businesses will have to reduce their emissions. Those that can't, because of cost or technical hurdles, can buy allowances from companies that have achieved cleaner emissions.

Companies have a financial incentive to curb emissions because they won't have to buy as many credits and because they can sell any they don't need. The price of credits is likely to rise as the cap is lowered. That gives companies more incentive to curb emissions sooner rather than later so they can buy and use credits at a lower price and sell them at a profit.

In addition, generators can make up for a small percentage of their emissions by purchasing narrowly defined carbon offsets, such as investing in energy-efficient building technology or planting trees to absorb carbon from the atmosphere.

The overal goal is to give utilities an economic incentive, rather than a regulatory mandate, to burn less coal, fuel oil and natural gas, while at the same time making carbon-free energy alternatives such as wind and solar power more economically attractive.

While power plants account for only a third of the carbon dioxide generated in the region, they're the easiest source to regulate because their emissions are already monitored in other pollution programs, said Peter Iwanowicz, director of the state Department of Environmental Conservation's Climate Change Office.

Eventually, the program may be expanded to include sources such as industry and transportation, Iwanowicz said.

Some business and utility leaders have urged the states to hold off until a national plan is developed.

The Business Council of New York State warns that the regional plan could harm the power supply and system reliability while increasing energy prices that are already 52 percent higher than the national average for commercial customers.

Research conducted by RGGI projects the typical New York residential customer will see an increase of 78 cents per month. But the Independent Power Producers of New York, an industry group, says the cost assumptions used by RGGI are outdated and inaccurate.

Not all energy generators oppose the plan.

"We're very much in favor of a national cap-and-trade system for reducing carbon emissions because we believe climate change is real and that it requires a national, and really international, solution," said Don McCloskey, environmental policy manager for Public Service Enterprise Group, a power generator in Newark, N.J.

While other carbon-curtailing programs have been proposed, including a carbon tax, McCloskey said PSEG supports cap-and-trade because it allows companies to use their ingenuity and knowledge of markets to achieve environmental goals.

He noted that while steep price increases were predicted when a similar program was launched to curb acid rain-causing sulfur dioxide emissions, the worst fears didn't come to pass.

The three-hour auction will be conducted online among previously approved bidders. At the end, bids in the system will be used to determine a clearing price based on supply versus demand. The minimum clearing price is set at $1.86 per ton for the first auction.

The big question is what the clearing price will end up being. In Europe, carbon trading has hit electric ratepayers hard, with carbon allowances selling for as high as $48 a ton at auction. RGGI has put plans in place to prevent the price from rising above $10 a ton.

Proceeds of the auctions are to be invested in programs to increase energy efficiency, support non-carbon-generating renewable energy sources such as wind and solar, and develop carbon abatement technologies.

Peter Cramton, a professor at the University of Maryland with a research focus on emissions auctions, said the RGGI program is a good basis for a national cap-and-trade program. But he said a national program should include all sources of carbon emissions, from automobiles to industries.

The auction isn't limited to electricity generators. Investors or public interest groups also may participate.

For the first auction, only one public interest group has registered in hopes of buying carbon credits and taking them out of circulation, Iwanowicz said.

"We want to purchase and retire some allowances," said John Sheehan, spokesman for the Adirondack Council, a group dedicated to preserving the wilderness character of the 6-million-acre Adirondack Park. "We set a goal of 1,000 tons initially."

Carbon dioxide emissions are of concern in the Adirondacks, Sheehan said, because global warming could transform the region's fragile boreal ecosystem to a temperate zone similar to Richmond, Va.

It's too early to say with certainty, but political analysts are seeing signs that the "Bush effect" may be fading, and Republican candidates -- particularly John Sununu -- might be shedding the albatross of the unpopular President.

Two years ago, Democrats swept to victory in New Hampshire and much of the nation by exploiting voter frustration with President George W. Bush. With the war in Iraq going poorly, Granite State voters, in a historic election, ousted two incumbent U.S. House members, replaced them with Democrats, and awarded Democrats with control of the state House and Senate, as well as the Executive Council.

Today, Democrats are banking on a replay. And in the one of the most-watched races in the country, they hope to keep the anti-Bush momentum going strong to replace Republican Sen. John Sununu with former Gov. Jeanne Shaheen.

"This election," said state Democratic Party senior adviser Kathy Sullivan, "is a referendum on the eight years of George Bush and the failures of his administration.

"Of course I see (the tie between Bush and Sununu) as an issue. John Sununu has been a big part of the problem. Shaheen and the state and national Democratic parties have relentlessly tied Sununu and other vulnerable senators across the country to Bush, whose New Hampshire approval rating was at 28 percent in a University of New Hampshire poll in May.

The Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee Web site's home page depicts Sununu and Bush standing shoulder to shoulder.

Shaheen recently called Sununu "complicit" with Bush in failing to provide funding for studies of new embryonic stem cells. She criticized him for backing Bush's veto of a big Medicare funding bill.

She was quoted as saying that Sununu, by voting with Bush in nine out of every 10 votes, "got us to where we are today: with $9 trillion in debt, a war in Iraq with no end, a housing crisis, gas prices that are the highest in history, and no energy plan."

Perhaps as a result of that line of attack, Sununu trailed badly in early UNH polling -- by 16 percentage points last fall, by 17 percentage points in February and by 12 percentage points in May.

Washington-based Rasmussen Reports on Friday released a poll showing Shaheen leading Sununu, 45 to 40 percent. Last month, the same pollster showed Shaheen leading 53 to 39 percent.

Why the closure?

Sununu has long tried to distance himself from Bush on certain issues in the Senate, with little success. His political answer to the Democrats is that he has voted with New Hampshire "100 percent of the time."

At the same time, the 2008 presidential election has taken over the national political spotlight. National news reports are consumed by Barack Obama and John McCain. Bush, a lame duck with five months left in his tenure, has been relegated to a less conspicuous place on the national political scene.

Escaping Bush's shadow

She was not surprised by the new UNH poll, she said.

As the race plays out, the nation -- not just the state -- will be watching.

WHY NEW HAMPSHIRE? The Granite State isn't the only state with a town called Unity.

We found perhaps an even more appropriately named town, Pleasant Unity, in the large swing state of Pennsylvania. That state also has a town called Unity House.

There is a Unity in Illinois, Barack Obama's home state and the state in which Hillary Clinton grew up. There are also Unitys in Maine, Oregon and Wisconsin. And there's a Unity in Ohio, which is one of the most important battleground states of all.

So, why New Hampshire? Because we're apparently special. With apologies to the state's detractors, we do have the most energized electorate in the country, thanks to the first-in-the-nation primary and the already geared-up general election battles. There's also the little coincidence that Obama and Clinton received an identical number of votes (107 each) in our Unity.

As much as the well-oiled machine that is the New Hampshire Democratic Party will deny it, the Obama-Clinton primary race left scars, and on one level, this is an attempt to soothe them and push for a win in a tough state in which John McCain is as well known, if not more well known, than any local politician.

The Obama campaign believes "they can win in New Hampshire and that if they pay attention to it now, they don't have to have as concentrated an effort down the road," says Manchester pollster Dick Bennett.

Although Sullivan County, with the blue-collar population centers of Claremont and Newport, may be viewed as "Clinton territory," in fact, Obama won the county in the primary, 3,823 to 3,512, although Clinton won Claremont, 1,114 to 898, and Newport, 470 to 422.

Bennett noted that his statewide poll last week had Obama leading McCain, 51 to 39 percent in New Hampshire, "and this appearance helps solidify the lead. Anything Hillary can do is a plus."

"It will be interesting to see what women at the event will say," Bennett said.

Obama's campaign told us in an e-mail this week, "New Hampshire is obviously going to be a critical state in the general election, and New Hampshire voters are incredibly energized and take great pride in helping to select our next President, as they have done for so many years. Senator Clinton and Senator Obama are looking forward to returning to a place where they competed vigorously ... "

WHO WILL BE ON STAGE? Since the Democrats have no meaningful state primaries for major offices, you can expect the entire top of the ticket on stage with Obama and Clinton, or nearby.

Jeanne Shaheen, Carol Shea-Porter, Paul Hodes, executive councilors and perhaps the entire state Senate lineup are expected to attend.

And super-delegate Gov. John Lynch will finally make it official tomorrow that he supports Obama, despite his recent friendly phone chat with McCain. First lady Dr. Susan Lynch, a top Clinton supporter, is also expected to attend.

WHAT ABOUT BILLY? Snarky question? Maybe, but: How far away will Bill Shaheen be from the Radisson Hotel Center of New Hampshire today when Michelle Obama and Jeane Shaheen campaign together? And will he be anywhere near Unity tomorrow?

The former state "First Hunk," you may remember, resigned last December as a Clinton campaign national and state co-chair after invoking Obama's past admitted use of illegal drugs into the campaign.

As the Clinton-Obama race reached the boiling point about three weeks before the primary, Bill told The Washington Post that if Obama became the nominee, the Republicans would "jump on his drug use.

"It'll be, 'When was the last time? Did you ever give drugs to anyone? Did you sell them to anyone?' There are so many openings for Republican dirty tricks. It's hard to overcome."

Bill Shaheen apologized profusely before resigning and hasn't been heard from in public since.

FREEDOM OR DUMMER? State GOP Chairman Fergus Cullen suggested, as McCain has, that Obama participate in joint town hall meetings with the Republican candidate, rather than holding rallies and giving speeches.

Cullen told Politico.com this week that "there is another small town in New Hampshire -- one called Freedom -- that I suppose the Obama campaign ruled out as a possible venue for Friday's event. That is because in an Obama presidency, we'd have less of it: less freedom to decide how to spend our money because more of it would be taken by the government in higher taxes; less freedom from foreign sources of energy because Obama opposes expanding domestic energy production; less freedom to make health care choices because Obama prefers big government health care to approaches that utilize the power of the free market to expand health care access and lower costs."

State Democratic Chairman Raymond Buckley commented, "Perhaps Fergus should move his office to the town of Dummer. His increasingly hysterical press releases just get dumber and dumber."

Monthly reports filed last Friday with the Federal Election Commission show that the state Democratic Party collected $208,000 in May, compared to $13,000 for the state GOP.

The state Democrats spent $135,199 in May and entered June with $165,412 on hand. The Republicans spent $14,898 in May and began this month with $104,055 on hand.

IT'S ABOUT TAXES. What drove Kathryn Forry, 67, a retired school teacher and education program coordinator from Jaffrey, to run for governor in a primary against ultra-popular two-term incumbent John Lynch?

Taxes.

In an interview this week, Forry, a soft-spoken Democrat who ran unsuccessfully for the state House four years ago, did not specifically advocate an income tax, but said, "We need a New Hampshire governor who will give leadership to the Legislature as our state chooses to reform the many unfair ways we tax ourselves in New Hampshire. We need to reduce the unjust property tax on our homes."

Forry, who said that 60 percent of her property taxes go to education, said New Hampshire needs "to establish a fair source of state revenue to reduce or replace some of the other unfair taxes we have. We need a governor who is willing and able to lead on this issue."

Forry said that through the business profits tax, the business enterprise tax, the interest and dividends tax and the property tax, "We have state income taxes that are paid only by some New Hampshire residents."

She said Lynch for two terms "has had a chance for leadership to address school funding, which has been festering since 1997, and he just hasn't done it. Others should now have a chance.

Forry said that while she likes Lynch, "he's not doing the job."

She and her husband have resided in New Hampshire for 40 years. Now retired, she was most recently the Title I director for the ConVal and Jaffrey-Rindge school districts.

"I just want them to stop the charade and talk about fair and just taxes for all of New Hampshire," she said.

Her approach? "I don't have a lot of money, but I'll be out there campaigning."

CALM DOWN. Here we go again, "sowing division among New Hampshire Democrats," as alleged on the Blue Hampshire Web site.

Our innocent, unassuming, straightforward report in this column last Sunday that Democrat Lynch telephoned Republican Sen. John McCain on June 11 to welcome him to the state caused a ruckus on the progressive Web log.

What's Lynch doing calling a Republican? How dare he call McCain? Whose side is he on, anyway?

Well, for the record, Lynch spokesman Colin Manning said Democrat Lynch will support Democrat Barack Obama with his super-delegate vote at the Denver convention.

"Senator Obama is the nominee and the governor is obviously going to support the nominee on the ticket. Of course he's going to support him for President," Manning said.

Oh, by the way, we understand the McCain campaign received several calls from conservatives already wary of McCain's credentials complaining about McCain being so nice to Lynch.

Can't we all just get along?
